DL 20: Other Metrics and the ROC Curve

This section covers why accuracy alone isn't enough to evaluate a binary classification model, especially with imbalanced datasets. It introduces a variety of metrics derived from a confusion matrix.

Key Metrics

  • Precision: How many of the positive predictions were actually correct?
  • Recall (True Positive Rate): How many of the actual positive cases were correctly identified?
  • F1 Score: The weighted average of Precision and Recall, providing a balanced measure.

The ROC Curve

The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve plots the True Positive Rate (TPR) against the False Positive Rate (FPR) at various classification thresholds. The closer the curve is to the top-left corner, the better the model.

DL 21: The Adam Optimizer

The Adam Optimizer (Adaptive Moment Estimation) is a highly effective optimization algorithm for training neural networks. It combines the benefits of Momentum and RMSprop.

How It Works

  • First Moment (Momentum): Maintains a moving average of past gradients to accelerate training in the right direction.
  • Second Moment (RMSprop): Maintains a moving average of squared gradients to adapt the learning rate for each parameter individually.

This adaptive learning rate is a key rule of Adam, allowing it to converge faster and more reliably than other methods.

DL 22: Auto-Encoders

An autoencoder is a neural network designed to learn a compressed representation of data. It consists of an encoder and a decoder.

Autoencoder Architecture

Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)

VAEs improve on autoencoders by producing a mean and a variance vector from the encoder, which creates a smooth, continuous latent space. This enables the model to reliably generate new data by sampling from this space.

DL 23: Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

A GAN is a generative model composed of two competing networks that are trained in an adversarial game.

  • Generator: Creates new, fake data samples from random noise.
  • Discriminator: Classifies whether an input is a real or fake data sample.

The rule of the game is for the Generator to produce fakes that can fool the Discriminator, while the Discriminator tries to improve its ability to detect them.

DL 24: AlphaGo & AlphaGo Zero

AlphaGo and AlphaGo Zero are AI systems from DeepMind that mastered the game of Go. Go's complexity makes it difficult for traditional AI.

AlphaGo's Networks

  • Policy Network: Narrows down the search space by predicting the most promising moves.
  • Value Network: Estimates the probability of winning from a given board state.

The AlphaGo Zero Rule

AlphaGo Zero learned Go entirely through self-play, without any human knowledge. It used a single neural network for both policy and value estimations, which was more efficient than the separate networks used by AlphaGo.

DL 26: Automatic Differentiation

Automatic Differentiation (AD) is the core technique for computing derivatives in deep learning.

Reverse Mode (Backpropagation)

This is the AD method used in deep learning. It's efficient because its complexity scales with the number of outputs (a single loss value).

The Gradient Tape Rule

During the forward pass, a "gradient tape" or computational graph is implicitly built. This tape records the operations and dependencies. During the backward pass, the derivatives are calculated by propagating them back through this recorded tape.
